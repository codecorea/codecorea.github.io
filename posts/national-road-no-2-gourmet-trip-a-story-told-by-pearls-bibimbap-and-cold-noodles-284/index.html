There are three major cold noodles on the Korean Peninsula. The main characters are Hamheung Langmyeon and Pyongyang Naengmyeon, which are familiar to us, and Pearl Noodles, which are relatively less known. The first two are from North Korea and Jinju Naengmyeon is the only South Korean. It is thanks to the ingredients that these cold noodles have become famous. Hamheung-do, located in the north of Gangwon-do, has lots of potatoes (instead of rice). Thanks to this, Hamheung Langmyeon using potato starch was born. The flounder, which is often caught on the beach in Hamheung, is spicy seasoned and eaten. The spicy seasoning is very different from Pyeongyang Naengmyeon, the soba noodles. Hamheung Nangmyeon (Hoeng Naengmyeon), which we now taste in this land, adds redfish sashimi and sweet potato starch instead of potatoes. During the Korean War, the refugees who came down to the south enjoyed the taste of their hometown by adding a lot of ingredients from this land.

In many ways, Pyeongyang Naengmyeon, which is compared to Jinju Naengmyeon, rolls the noodles made of buckwheat flour into cold Naengmyeon soup. Pyongyang is the capital of North Korea and the center of cultural economy. It has a wide field and the Yellow Sea, so it is a place to enjoy eating abundant fish and fruit. The food was plentiful, so the drinking spirit would have developed. The fact that “Pyongyang Gisaeng” has been famous since ancient times will not have anything to do with it. If you've ever tasted hot and cold noodle, it's Pyongyang Naengmyeon. The characteristic of Pyongyang Naengmyeon is its light taste. This also spread throughout the country after the Korean War and became a delicacy of the four seasons.
